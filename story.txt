## The Lighthouse Keeper's Last Signal

### Chapter 1: The Storm

Maren had not spoken to another living soul in forty-three days. That was fine by her. The lighthouse on Vael Rock needed tending, and she was the only one stubborn enough to do it.
The autumn storms came early that year. By October, the sea had turned the color of iron, and the wind screamed through the gallery railing like something wounded. Maren climbed the spiral stairs twice each night to check the lamp, her boots ringing against the metal steps in a rhythm she'd come to find comforting. It was the heartbeat of the place.
On the forty-fourth night, the storm was different. It didn't build gradually the way coastal weather usually did — it arrived all at once, as if the sky had simply collapsed. Rain lashed the windows sideways. The beam from the lantern room cut through the dark in stuttering arcs, catching the spray of waves that crashed halfway up the tower.
Maren was in the watch room when she saw it: a light, far out on the water, blinking in a pattern she recognized. Three short, three long, three short. An old distress signal — one that hadn't been used in decades.
She pressed her face to the glass. The light blinked again, patient and deliberate, from somewhere beyond the reef. No modern vessel would use that signal. No modern vessel would be anywhere near Vael Rock in weather like this.
She reached for the radio. Static. She tried again. Nothing but the hiss of dead air.
The light blinked on.

### Chapter 2: The Answer

Maren told herself she wouldn't go down to the shore. It was reckless, the rocks were slick, and the tide was high enough to pull a person under in seconds. She told herself this while lacing her boots and pulling on her oilskin coat.
The path down to the water was barely a path at all — a series of handholds carved into the cliff face by some keeper long before her. The rain made every surface treacherous. She descended slowly, her lantern swinging from one hand, casting wild shadows across the stone.
At the base of the cliff, where the rocks met the churning surf, she stopped. The blinking light was closer now, maybe two hundred meters out. She raised her own lantern and swung it in the old reply pattern: steady, steady, flash.
For a long moment, nothing happened. Then the light on the water went dark.
Maren waited. The wind tore at her coat. The sea roared and withdrew, roared and withdrew.
When the light reappeared, it was no longer on the water. It hovered just above the surface, drifting toward her, and she could see now that it had no source — no boat, no flare, no lantern. Just a pale glow moving with quiet purpose through the storm.
It stopped at the edge of the rocks. In its faint illumination, Maren saw the outline of a hand, raised in greeting.
She did not run. She raised her own hand in return.
Some signals, she understood, were not meant to be explained — only answered.
